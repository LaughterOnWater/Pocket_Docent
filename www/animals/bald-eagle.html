Our National Symbol

You might just occasionally see one of these magnificent birds in the wild right in Clarke County! You're most likely to see them while on the river or at the Botanical Gardens. You can learn more than the casual observer about our Bald Eagles by clicking the buttons below...

Taxonomy

Order Accipitriformes
Family Accipitridae
Genus Haliaeetus
Species leucocephalus

Diet

  • In the wild, bald eagles eat mostly fish, but will also capture and eat birds, small mammals, and scavenge carrion (especially in winter) that they may find.
  • At Bear Hollow, they receive a rotation of rats and whole fish.

Range and Habitat

  • Bald eagles can be found across the entire continent of North America, absent only from extreme northern Canada.
  • In Georgia, bald eagles are found as year-round residents in the southern half of the state.  In the northern half of the state, they tend to be winter migrants, but may occasionally be seen throughout the year.
  • Habitats include anywhere with significant amounts of water; coastal areas, lakes, rivers, and marshes are all good choices.  It is considered as ‘Sea Eagle’.  Old growth, mature forested areas are also needed.
  • They are extremely sensitive to human activity, and is found most commonly in areas free of human disturbance.

Physical Features

  • Body is between 28 and 42 inches in length.  Adults weigh around 6 – 8 pounds (females tend to be just a bit larger).  Their bones are so light because they are hollow, enabling them to fly.  Wingspan is immense – up to eight feet from wingtip to wingtip.
  • Adults have a brilliant white head and tail.  Bright yellow eyes, beak and feet contrast against a dark brown body.
  • Powerful flyer. Reaches speeds of 35 – 43 mph while flapping and gliding and can reach speeds of 75-99mph when diving!
  • An eagle's eye is almost as large as a human's, but its sharpness is at least four times that of a person with perfect vision.

Life Span

  • In the wild, bald eagles often life up to 25 or 30 years.
  • In captivity, bald eagles can live 35 to nearly 40 years, and at least one captive eagle lived to be 48!

Reproduction

  • Reach maturity at four or five years of age – the white head and tail are then completely filled in.
  • Breeding occurs in the winter, and one to three eggs are laid shortly thereafter.  Both males and females will incubate the eggs.
  • Not all of the chicks will survive to fledging though.

Social Behavior

  • Tend to be monogamous.  However, if the pair cannot produce offspring or if one mate dies, they will look for a new partner.
  • Courtship flight involves incredible aerial acrobatics – dives, cartwheels, free falling, talon locking.
  • Can often be seen in large groups, numbering up to 100 individuals at a time.

Conservation

  • Bald eagles were once an endangered species due to the wide use of DDT a chemical used in agriculture which was not fatal to adults but could cause them to be infertile and weakened the shells of eggs such that they broke before the chick was ready to hatch.  Thankfully, they have fully recovered since the banning of DDT. 
  • They were officially reclassified from ‘Endangered’ to ‘Threatened’ on July 12, 1995.  Officially removed from the Federal list of endangered and threatened wildlife on June 28, 2007. 
  • However, they are still completely protected since they are our national emblem and a bird of prey.

 Interpretive Information

  • Nests are the largest of any North American bird.  They can be thirteen feet deep, eight feet across and weigh well over a ton.
  • Our nation’s emblem almost wasn’t according to popular legend.  Ben Franklin wanted to nominate the wild turkey instead of the bald eagle.  However, according to Wikipedia, there may not be as much truth to this legend. 
    • “Contrary to popular legend, there is no evidence that Benjamin Franklin ever publically supported the Wild Turkey, rather than the Bald Eagle, as a symbol of the United States. The origin of this claim is a letter Franklin wrote to his daughter in 1784 from Paris. However, this letter was a criticism of the Society of the Cincinnati in which he stated his personal distaste for the Bald Eagle's behavior but he does not mention publically disputing the choice of the Bald Eagle for the Great Seal of the United States. In the letter Franklin states: ‘For my own part. I wish the Bald Eagle had not been chosen the Representative of our Country. He is a Bird of bad moral Character. He does not get his Living honestly...Besides he is a rank Coward: The little King Bird not bigger than a Sparrow attacks him boldly and drives him out of the District.’”
  • Bald eagles are considered sacred by many Native American tribes.  Eagles are considered spiritual messengers between gods and humans by some cultures.  A lone Eagle feather is thought to convey great power.

Bear Hollow’s Bald Eagles

  • Female: Amazon (larger):
    • It’s estimated that she hatched in the wild around 1982.
    • She was found with a wing injury in 1987.
    • Rehabilitated at the Raptor Center at Auburn University.  She had a partial right wing amputation because of a collision, most likely from a power line.
    • Arrived at Bear Hollow on October 7, 2002.
  • Male: Hanovi
    • Hanovi allegedly translates to “Strong”.  In researching it, I first found on a website that Hanovi meant “Deer” in an unspecified Native American language.  Ultimately, I discovered that Hanovi was the pseudonym of a famous Native American fiction author, but I did find a reference that Hanovi (alternately spelled Honovi) means “Strong Deer” in the Hopi language.
    • He was born in the wild and came to the zoo after being rehabilitated in the summer of 2012. Estimates are that he might have been born around 2005.
