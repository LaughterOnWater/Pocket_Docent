Silent Night Flyers...

They have special feathers that allow them to fly almost silently. Their ear orientation is such that they can locate prey by hearing in any direction. You can learn more than the casual observer by clicking the buttons below...

Taxonomy

Order Strigiformes
Family Tytonidae
Genus Tyto
Species alba

Diet

  • In the wild, barn owls will eat a variety of prey items including small mammals, birds, large insects, small lizards, snakes and frogs.
  • At Bear Hollow, they receive a rotation of mice, quail and chicks.

Range and Habitat

  • The barn owl occurs globally.  It lives throughout the entire continents of North and South America and Australia and lives through the majority of Europe and the Middle East.  It also occurs throughout southeast Asia and almost all of Africa, the only exceptions being the middle of the Congo and the Sahara desert.
  • Found statewide in Georgia.
  • Prefers open country like farmlands or grasslands with occasional patches of forested areas.

Physical Features

  • Large, pale colored owl.  Usually a beige or tan color with darker sprinkles across the back and chest.  Heart shaped faced is usually white with dark eyes and a dark line connecting the eyeslits to the beak.
  • Long, bare legs with strongly curved talons on the toes.  Short tail, often not noticed when the wings are folded.
  • Body length is between thirteen and fifteen inches.  Wingspan is between 30 and 40 inches from wingtip to wingtip.  Weight is generally right at one pound.

Life Span

  • In the wild, barn owls usually life for around two years.
  • In captivity, they can live up to ten or eleven years.

Reproduction

  • Barn owls prefer to nest in old buildings, caves and wells but will also nest in hollow trees.
  • Will breed at any time of the year, but winter and early spring tend to be the most common for courtship activity.
  • Chicks hatch after bout one month of incubation.  They leave the nest at about two months of age and learn to hunt on their own.

Social Behavior

  • Not well documented, but probably a little more tolerant of other owls.
  • Often show partnership in hunting, particularly between mated pairs or siblings.

Conservation

  • A species of little conservation concern, they seem to be fairly common throughout their range.  However there are a few locations where they are not as numerous as they had been previously.
  • Like other raptors, barn owls are completely protected by state and federal laws.

 Interpretive Information

  • Barn owls have a higher metabolic rate than other birds of prey, and they consume many more prey items than a similarly sized bird.  This may make them the most economically valuable non-domesticated animal to humans.
  • Owl ears are placed in different positions on their head.  This allows them to pinpoint exactly where a sound is coming from, even in complete darkness.
  • Barn owls do not hoot like other owls, but instead make a hissing noise or a very loud screech.
