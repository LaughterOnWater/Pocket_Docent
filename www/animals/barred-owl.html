Barred Owls

Taxonomy

Order Strigiformes
Family Strigidae
Genus Strix
Species varia

Diet

  • In the wild, they are considered ‘generalists’. They eat fish, frogs, crayfish and other wetland animals, in addition to small mammals and birds.
  • At Bear Hollow, they receive a rotation of large mice, quail, chicks and rats.

Range and Habitat

  • Barred owls occur throughout the entire eastern and central portions of North America.  There is also a small band that goes south into central Mexico, and a large swath that cuts across Canada and into the Pacific Northwest and northwest Canada.
  • They occur statewide in Georgia.
  • Habitats include dense mesic forests, swamps, and forested areas bordering wetlands.

Physical Features

  • Large, brownish-gray owl.  Rounded head and facial disk.  Thick brown barring across the chest and stomach area.  Wings and tail are heavily barred and checked with white.
  • Large, dark eyes and a yellow beak give the barred owl an unusual look.
  • Body length is between 16 and 25 inches.  Wingspan from tip to tip is between 38 and 50 inches.  Males tend to weigh around a pound and a half, females are closed to two and a half pounds.

Life Span

  • In the wild, barred owls have lived up to ten years.
  • In captivity, they can live up to 23 years.

Reproduction

  • Courtship begins in February with the males calling loudly and establishing territories.
  • Breeding occurs later in March and into Spring.  They lay two to four eggs in a large hollow tree or abandoned hawk nest.
  • The eggs are incubated for about a month, and the babies leave the nest approximately forty days later.

Social Behavior

  • Barred owl pairs are monogamous and stay mated for life.
  • Usually solitary hunters, they may be found together occasionally, particularly during the breeding season.

Conservation

  • A species of little conservation concern, they seem to be common and widespread their range. 
  • Like other raptors, barn owls are completely protected by state and federal laws.

 Interpretive Information

  • Barred owls are strong callers.  A deep resonating, eight note hooting call, ending in a rolling ‘wah’.  The call is often liked to them saying “Who cooks for you?  Who cooks for you all?”
  • When a calling frenzy gets going, the owl group sounds like a troop of monkeys cackling at each other.
