Black Bears in Athens

We're not likely to see a Black Bear in Athens, though in the very north-east tip of Georgia, you may ocassionally find them coming into mountain towns when food and water are scarce in the forest. You can learn more than the casual observer by clicking the buttons below...

Taxonomy

Order Carnivora
Family Ursidae
Genus Ursus
Species americanus

Diet

  • In the wild, bears eat herbs, nuts, grasses, berries, insects and their larvae, and a little animal matter. Occasionally preys on small animals, rarely deer and livestock.
  • At Bear Hollow, they receive a mix of fruits and vegetables, dog chow, and peanut butter.
  • Occasionally, you’ll see an enrichment object in their exhibit (like a large PVC pipe with food items inside, or a cardboard box, a pumpkin or food items stuffed in the big tires).  These enrichment objects are designed to give them a challenge, something new, and to force them to forage for their food in a way that simulates their time in the wild.

Range and Habitat

  • Wooded areas primarily.  Also live in mountains, swamps and coastal areas.
  • In North America, occurs in 41 of 50 states.
  • In Georgia, distribution is patchy.  Most populations are in the mountain and coastal plain regions, but occasional wandering into the piedmont is not uncommon.

Physical Features

  • Thick black fur covers the body.  Snout is generally brownish.  Short, rounded ears.
  • Can be five to six feet in length, and 2.5 to three feet tall at the shoulder.
  • Females weight between 90 and 400 lbs.  Males weigh between 250 and 600 lbs.
  • Hind legs are slightly longer than fore legs.  Has 42 teeth.
  • They usually walk with a shuffling gait, but can run at speeds of over 30 mph. 
  • They are agile climbers.  Look at the trees in the bear exhibit.  Branches that are broken or pointing downward indicate where the bears have climbed. 
  • Dexterous toes and lips and a long tongue enable American black bears to gather berries and insects they dig up after ripping open rotting logs or honey combs with their strong, curved claws.

Life Span

  • Black bears can live anywhere from 21-34 years. 
  • In the wild, America black bears can live up to 32 years.

Reproduction

  • Reach breeding maturity at 3 or 4 years of age.  However, males may not be strong enough to win breeding rights for several more years.
  • Mating generally occurs from June to August, and the two or three cubs are born in January or February the following year.
  • Cubs become independent their second summer.

Social Behavior

  • Black bear activity periods shift during the year, but they are mostly diurnal.
  • Generally timid and prefer to flee than to have a confrontation.  Black bears seldom attack, expect when they are injured, or feel cornered or threatened.

Conservation

  • Black bears are a species of least concern.  In fact, most populations in the eastern US seem to be increasing steadily.
  • Only the isolated Louisiana, Texas and Mississippi populations are listed as threatened by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service. However, other unlisted but scattered populations, such as those in Florida, are vulnerable to poaching, habitat loss, and roadkill deaths.  Some estimates put the continent’s black bear population at about a half million.
  • While black bears themselves are not currently endangered, there are serious concerns because of the large demand in Asian markets both in the US and abroad for bear bile, bile taken from bear gallbladders purported to have medicinal qualities.  Overharvesting of bears for their gallbladders has decimated bear populations in Asia, particularly the Asiatic Black Bear.  There are growing concerns of the legal and illegal harvesting of American Black Bears to meet the demand in Asian markets.  Thankfully, conservation efforts in the United States are working to ensure that American Black Bears do not follow the course of the rest of the world’s bears where six of the total eight bear species are threatened with extinction.

 Interpretive Information

  • Smokey the Bear is based off of an actual black bear cub found in New Mexico after a forest fire.
  • Morris Michtom, the creator of the teddy bear, was inspired to make the toy when he came across a cartoon of Theodore Roosevelt refusing to shoot a black bear cub trapped up a tree.   
  • Winnie the Pooh was named after Winnipeg, a female black bear cub that lived at London Zoo from 1915 until her death in 1934. (Pictured right).  Christopher Milne had named his toy bear after Winnie, a Canadian black bear which he often saw at London Zoo, and "Pooh", a swan they had met while on holiday. The bear cub was purchased from a hunter for $20 by Canadian Lieutenant Harry Colebourn in White River, Ontario, Canada, while en route to England during the First World War. He named the bear "Winnie" after his adopted hometown in Winnipeg, Manitoba. "Winnie" was surreptitiously brought to England with her owner, and gained unofficial recognition as The Fort Garry Horse regimental mascot. Colebourn left Winnie at the London Zoo while he and his unit were in France; after the war she was officially donated to the zoo, as she had become a much loved attraction there
  • Male bears (bruins) tend to wander far and wide.  Females (sows) tend to remain close to the area in which they were born.
  • Each paw has five long claws.  They are used to tear through logs for food and help the bear climb trees.  You can direct guests to look at the trees in the bear yard.  All of the tree limbs that are pointing/angled downwards are that way because of bears climbing them! 
  • Some guests will ask you if DJ and one of the females might reproduce.  All of the bears are spayed/neutered so that they cannot reproduce.  We are not a breeding facility and breeding animals would not serve our mission. 

Bear Hollow’s Black Bears

  • Male:  DJ
    • Born wild in January 2003, but raised at Ellijay Wildlife Rehabilitation Sanctuary.  He was probably orphaned when he was about two months old.
    • Arrived at Bear Hollow in late Sept. 2006.  He is still growing but currently is about 6 ft. tall and weighs nearly 400 lbs.
  • Females: Athena (pictured above) and Yonah
    • Sisters born in the Spring of 2010. 
    • Athena has a white patch on her chest and Yonah does not.  It is roughly crescent moon shaped and is a coloration that occurs in only 25% of American Black Bears; however, it is constant in Asiatic Black Bears. The Asiatic black bear is the American black bear's closest cousin. Both are thought to have evolved from a common European ancestor.
    • Raised at Bear Hollow by staff and volunteers after their mother was killed by poachers.
  • Past Bears:
    • Josie – Female bear born in captivity in 1988.  She was donated to Bear Hollow by the Humane Society of Cleveland, Ohio in 1990.  DJ’s former cagemate passed away in early summer of 2010 due to a degenerative spinal condition. She was 22 years old.
