Bobcats in Athens

Bobcats and humans live in a similar landscape: mountains, hills or plains. However, there is only a remote possibility of spotting a bobcat in and around Athens, Georgia. You can learn more than the casual observer by clicking the buttons below...

Taxonomy

Order Carnivora
Family Felidae
Genus Lynx
Species rufus

Diet

  • In the wild, bobcats eat rabbits, squirrels, rats, mice, fish, insects and small reptiles. They are opportunistic predators, and they can prey on animals as large as deer.  The bobcat is able to go for long periods without food, but will eat heavily when prey is abundant. During lean periods, it will often prey on larger animals that it can kill and return to feed on later.
  • At Bear Hollow, they eat mice and rats, chicken wings, fish occasionally, as well as a prepared carnivore diet.

Range and Habitat

  • Cosmopolitan.  Bobcats inhabit forested areas, semi deserts, urban edges, swamps and may other habitats.
  • In North America, occurs most everywhere except in a small region of the Midwest and North-central states.
  • In Georgia, Bobcats are found statewide.

Physical Features

  • About twice as large as a domestic cat.
  • Light brown or tawny colored fur.  Darker spots may or may not be present.
  • Has large tufted ears, with a black and white spot on the back of each ear.
  • Prominent black bars on the forelegs, and a black tipped, stubby tail.
  • Females and males have the same coloring, but males are generally larger.  Males range from 12-40 lbs., with an average of 18-28 lbs., while females range from 9-34 lbs. and average 14-20 lbs.

Life Span

  • In the wild, bobcats tend to live about 10 to 12 years.
  • In captivity, they can live up to 18 or 20 years, and some have reached 30 years.

Reproduction

  • Dominant males will follow females until they are reproductively receptive.  Both sexes may mate with multiple partners.
  • Breeds from winter into spring.  Kittens are born around two months later.

Social Behavior

  • Bobcats are territorial and largely solitary, though there may be some overlap in home ranges.
  • Territory edges are defined using claw scraping or scent marking (urine or feces).
  • Primarily crepuscular (active at dawn and dusk) in warmer months, but may become more diurnal in the fall and winter.
  • It’s an excellent climber and will swim when necessary, but will generally avoid water.

Conservation

  • A species of little conservation concern.  Populations have proven resilient and secure over most of the range.  The bobcat is listed in Appendix II of the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES), which means it is not considered threatened with extinction, but that hunting and trading must be closely monitored.
  • Most states allow a hunting season for bobcats, and at one time their pelts were worth a high price.

 Interpretive Information

  • Bobcats stick to their well-defined territories.  Territories vary in size depending on sex of the bobcat and prey availability.
  • In Native American stories, bobcats are associated with the fog in conjunction with Coyote who is associated with the wind.  Bobcats feature heavily in many Native American stories and traditions.
  • They generally avoid areas with high human disturbance and are rarely nuisance problems.
