Corn Snake
(Red Rat Snake)  

Taxonomy

Order Squamata
Family Colubridae
Genus Elaphe
Species guttata

Diet

  • Young hatchlings tend to feed on lizards and tree frogs, while adults feed on larger prey, such as mice, rats, birds, and bats.
  • At Bear Hollow, they are fed mice, rats, and chicks.

Range and Habitat

  • Corn snakes are found in the eastern United States from southern New Jersey south through Florida, west into Louisiana and parts of Kentucky. However, corn snakes are most abundant in Florida and the southeastern U.S.
  • Corn snakes may be found in wooded groves, rocky hillsides, meadowlands, woodlots, barns, and abandoned buildings.

Physical Features

  • Corn snakes are slender with a length of 24 to 72 inches. They are usually orange or brownish yellow, with large, black-edged red blotches down the middle of the back.
  • Considerable variation occurs in the coloration and patterns of individual snakes, depending on the age of the snake and the region of the country in which it is found. Hatchlings lack much of the bright coloration found on adults.

Life Span

  • The life span of this snake is up to 23 years in captivity but is generally much less in the wild.

Reproduction

  • The breeding season of corn snakes is from March to May. The snakes are oviparous, meaning they produce eggs that mature and hatch after being expelled, depositing a clutch of ten to 30 eggs in late May to July. Eggs are laid in rotting stumps, piles of decaying vegetation, or other similar locations where there is sufficient heat and humidity to incubate them. The adult snakes do not care for the eggs.
  • Once laid, the gestation period of the eggs is 60 to 65 days at about 82° F. The eggs hatch sometime in July through September. Hatchlings are 10 to 15 inches long and mature in 18 to 36 months.

Social Behavior

  • These snakes are generally solitary in the wild, but in captivity they can be housed with snakes of similar size and habits.

Conservation

  • Corn snakes for the most part are locally abundant.  However, they are listed by several states because they face habitat loss and destruction.
  • Corn snakes are often mistaken for copperheads and killed. 

Interpretive Information

  • The name corn snake is believed to have originated from the similarity of the markings on the belly to the checkered pattern of kernels of maize or Indian corn.
  • They are also sometimes called the red rat snake.
  • Corn snakes are primarily diurnal (active during the day). They readily climb trees and enter abandoned buildings in search of prey. However, they are very secretive and spend most of their time underground prowling through rodent burrows. They also often hide under loose bark and beneath logs, rocks, and other debris during the day.
