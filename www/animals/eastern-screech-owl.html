Eastern Screech Owl

Masters of camouflage, these feathered friends come in red and gray forms or "morphs". When perfectly still against the right tree bark, they can be almost impossible to see.

Taxonomy

Order Strigiformes
Family Strigidae
Genus Megascops
Species asio

Diet

  • In the wild, Eastern Screech-Owls eat most kinds of small animals, including birds and mammals as well as surprisingly large numbers of invertebrates, including earthworms, insects, crayfish, tadpoles, frogs, and lizards.
  • At Bear Hollow, the Eastern Screech-Owls eat a ration of rats, and baby chicks.

Range and Habitat

  • Eastern Screech-Owls are found throughout East North America from East Montana and the Great Lakes to the Gulf of Mexico, South to Tamaulipas in Northeast Mexico; also South Ontario to Florida.
  • In Georgia, screech owls are found statewide.
  • They do not have a preferred habitat type, but are generalists. They can be found in open hardwood, pine and northern coniferous forest, swamps, mountains and in suburban areas like back yards and parks. Almost any habitat with sufficient tree cover will do for this cosmopolitan owl.
  • Tree cavities or nest boxes are essential, and fairly open understories are preferred, but Eastern Screech-Owls live and breed successfully in farmland, suburban landscapes, and city parks.

Physical Features

  • The Eastern Screech-Owl is a short, stocky bird, with a large head and almost no neck. Its body length is between 6 - 10 inches, and its weight is about the weight of a hamburger patty.
  • Their wings are rounded, and their wingspan is between 19 - 24 inches.
  • Their tail is short and square and their pointed ear tufts are often raised, lending its head a distinctive silhouette. 
  • Eastern Screech-Owls can be either mostly gray or mostly reddish-brown (rufous). Whatever the overall color, they are patterned with complex bands and spots that give the bird excellent camouflage against tree bark.
  • Their eyes are yellow, and they have short legs, usually hidden under their feathers.
  • They also have 2 small tufts of feathers on top of their head.  These are not ears though people often think that they are.

Life Span

  • While captive Eastern Screech Owls have lived for over 20 years, wild birds would be unlikely to reach this age.  Juvenile and adult mortality may be as high as 70% and 30% respectively.
  • Predators of these Owls include Great Horned Owls, Barred Owls, Long-eared Owls, Great gray Owls, Short-eared Owls, Snowy Owls, mink, weasels, raccoons, skunks, snakes, crows, and Blue Jays.

Reproduction

  • The average age for sexual maturity for both male and female screech owls is one year.
  • Breading season for Eastern Screech Owls is generally around mid April, but may range from mid March to mid May.
  • Nests are usually found in natural tree cavities, hollow stumps, and limbs; nests have been found up to 50 feet above the ground. Remnant materials are formed into a depression, instead of building a nest in the cavity. They also use nest boxes.
  • The clutch size is usually 3-5 white to off-white eggs, with an incubation period of 26-30 days.
  • Usually having 1 brood a year, the eastern screech owl may have a second brood if the first is lost.

Social Behavior

  • Screech Owls are primarily solitary, with each individual having its own staked out territory. They may congregate to inspect a newcomer or when breeding season begins.
  • Most owl pairs, of about the same age, will bond for life. Male screech owls execute elaborate courtship displays. To advertise a nest site he will trill, hop, bow, bill snap and fill the nest with food. A female will usually choose a nest site that has been well supplied by the male. They will nest together during mating season and throughout the winter. Individuals will replace a mate that has disappeared.
  • At the end of the breeding season, the parents force the young out of their birth territory. Little is known about when this is done, but it appears to be affected by weather, food availability, and population density.

Conservation

  • Screech Owls are of little conservation concern. They seem to be very common across their range, and are often found in backyards in suburban areas.
  • Like other birds of prey, they are completely protected by both state and federal laws.

 Interpretive Information

  • Screech owls do not hoot, they have a whinny-like call. There are several variations: some ascend in pitch, others descend, and others do not waver (this is called a ‘tremolo’).
  • The true function of the two tufts of feathers on their head is unknown, but it is speculated that they help break up the outline of the owl when it is resting.
  • When prey is plentiful, Eastern Screech-Owls cache extra food in tree holes for as long as four days.
