Gopher Frogs in Athens

They don't normally live this far north, so you're not likely to see them in the wilds of Athens, GA. However they are a native species of southern Georgia and live in the burrows of Gopher Tortoises. You can learn more than the casual observer by clicking the buttons below...

Taxonomy

Order Anura
Family Ranidae
Genus Rana
Species capito

Diet

  • In the wild, adult gopher frogs feed on invertebrates, as well as on other anurans, especially toads.
  • At Bear Hollow, the frogs eat crickets.

Range and Habitat

  • They are mainly found on the Coastal Plain of the Southeastern United States.
  • The gopher frog usually spends daylight hours in burrows, holes, or tunnels that are created by other animals. In sandhill habitats, they frequently seek refuge in gopher tortoise burrows.
  • In Georgia, the gopher frog is restricted to longleaf pine ecosystems. It occurs in sandy and well drained, longleaf pine - saw palmetto - wiregrass sandhills as well as more poorly drained longleaf pine flatwoods.  Gophers are a resident in the sandhills, particularly of the longleaf pine-wiregrass ecosystem.
  • They need open areas with sandy soil and lots of grasses and other vegetation to forage.

Physical Features

  • The gopher frog is a stout, medium-sized frog with adults ranging from 6.5 - 10.8 cm (2½ - 4¼ inches) in length.
  • Dorsal coloration is variable and may be brown, gray, or creamy white with numerous reddish brown or dark brown spots. Warts are prominent on the dorsum. Two raised ridges (dorsolateral folds), often tinged with a yellow or brassy color, run down the back, one reaching from behind each eye to the hind limb. These raised ridges are one of the key identifiers.
  • The belly is white or cream-colored with dark mottling in most Georgia populations. There is often yellow on the underside of the hind limbs.

Life Span

  • Life expectancy ranges from six to ten years.
  • Males are mature at four to six months. Females mature in two to three years. 

Reproduction

  • In Georgia, the gopher frog is restricted to longleaf pine ecosystems. It occurs in sandy and well drained, longleaf pine - saw palmetto - wiregrass sandhills as well as more poorly
    drained longleaf pine flatwoods.
  • The wetlands used by breeding gopher frogs are typically ephemeral (seasonally dry) and always lack larger, predatory fish species.
  • Gopher frogs typically migrate to breeding ponds in the fall, winter, and early spring in association with heavy rains. Adults spend a variable period of time at the breeding pond; males typically remain at ponds longer than females. Adult male frogs call from shallow water along the shoreline, while floating on the surface of the water in deep areas, or while submerged. The call is of very low frequency and can carry a considerable distance. Females attach large egg masses (up to the size of a grapefruit) to emergent vegetation near the water’s surface. Egg masses may contain 2,000 or more individual eggs. Average length of time to hatching is approximately a week. Metamorphosis follows an 87-215 day developmental period.

Social Behavior

  • Gopher frogs are extremely secretive so little appears to be known about their social behavior beyond their breeding habits and the fact that they live in gopher tortoise burrows or abandoned mammal burrows.

Conservation

  • Largely due to the drastic loss of this ecosystem, as well as to the embedded isolated wetlands necessary for breeding and larval development, gopher frog populations have significantly declined throughout their range. Listed as "rare" in Georgia, extant populations in the state are known from only six sites. Because of the highly fragmented landscape throughout the gopher frog's historic range, it is very unlikely that the species will recolonize restored lands without the help of repatriation efforts. It is likely that the decline of gopher tortoise populations has adversely affected this frog.
  • The Atlanta Botanical Garden, in partnership with the University of Georgia, Georgia Department of Natural Resources, the Nature Conservancy, Jones Ecological Research Center, and Zoo Atlanta, collects portions of wild Gopher Frog egg masses so that they can be reared in captivity. The resulting tadpoles are safely grown in Atlanta Botanical Garden facilities until they metamorphose into froglets. At this point they are transported to a Nature Conservancy preserve in southwestern Georgia that has all of the right environmental conditions, but no Gopher Frogs. These released frogs have also been marked with a special acrylic elastomer, basically a frog tattoo, so that if they are recaptured in the future, biologists will know when they were released.

 Interpretive Information

  • While they are not federally protected, they are considered “Rare” in Georgia and various protected populations exist.
  • This is a perfect animal to talk about in conjunction with the Gopher Tortoise as this is one of the many species that relies on the Gopher Tortoise for its own existence.

Bear Hollow’s Gopher Frogs

  •  UGA and the State Botanical Gardens were doing a conservation project where they would go to some place east of Macon and they collected egg masses out of the wild.  They divided them up between approximately four different facilities and ours was one of them.  Bear Hollow staff fostered them and then returned them to UGA when they were a certain age and then UGA released them to certain sites in the wild.  Ours was the one that produced the most, in large part due to Tom’s careful husbandry of them.  Ask Tom if you have any questions about them!
