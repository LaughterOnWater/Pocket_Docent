Great Horned Owls in Athens

It's entirely possible to see and hear these magnificent owls in the wilds of Athens, GA. You can learn more than the casual observer by clicking the buttons below...

Taxonomy

Order Strigiformes
Family Strigidae
Genus Bubo
Species virginiana

Diet

  • In the wild, great horned owls eat a variety of mammals, like mice, rats, rabbits, squirrels, skunks, raccoons, and other small to medium sized prey.  Birds are also taken and occasionally snakes or frogs.
  • At Bear Hollow, the great horned owls eat a rotation of chicks, small rats and mice.

Range and Habitat

  • An incredibly wide ranging owl.  They can be found across the entire North American continent, just south of the arctic tree-line, down through Central America and in a broad swatch across central South America.
  • They occur statewide in Georgia.
  • Habitats include both deciduous and coniferous forests, swampland, mountains, grasslands and urban areas.

Physical Features

  • Very large, stocky owl.  Feathers are brownish or grayish, with light and dark stippling and spotting.  Often have orangeish highlights.
  • Facial disks are most often brownish orange and eyes are big and bright yellow.  Two large father tufts on top of the head.
  • Body length is 18 to 27 inches.  Wingspan is between 48 and 60 inches from wingtip to wingtip.  Great horned owls usually weigh around 3 lbs. as adults.

Life Span

  • In the wild, great horned owls can live up to thirteen years of age.
  • In captivity, it is not unheard of for a great horned owl to live into their 30’s.  The record is over 38 years.

Reproduction

  • Pairs begin courtship in late fall.  Breeding season is in December, and the two eggs are usually laid in January.
  • Nests are either taken over from squirrels, crows or small hawks, or they may create their own in a large hollow tree, cave or protected rooftop.
  • The eggs are incubated for about 33 days, and the young then leave the nest after about 21 days.  Young owlets stay with their parents until he start of the new breeding season.

Social Behavior

  • Great horned owls that have formed mated pairs are permanent residents within their territories.
  • However, they do not often stay together except during the breeding season.  They will share a territory but remain apart in their separate areas.

Conservation

  • A species of little conservation concern.  Populations seem to be common and secure.
  • However, they are completely protected by both state and federal laws.

 Interpretive Information

  • Owls’ ears are not symmetrical.  The right ear opening is usually set higher than the left, and it tends to open at a different angle too.  Often it may be a different size as well. 
  • The typical ‘hoot owl’, great horned owls have a five syllable call: ‘hoo-hoo hoooo hoo-hoo’ rapid at first, then slower and lower for the last two syllables.  It is often likened to “Who’s awake?  Me, too.”  Males have a deeper voice than the females.
  • Great horned owls are considered to be the nocturnal counterpart of the red tailed hawks.

Bear Hollow’s Great Horned Owls

  • Male:  Eeyore
    • Hatched in the wild at an unknown time.  Rehabilitated at Carolina Raptor Center and arrived at Bear Hollow in 2000.
  • Female: Winnie the Hoo
    • Hatched in the wild at an unknown time.  Hit by car in Indiana and rode all the way to Athens on the grill of the car.  Taken to the UGA vet hospital and rehabilitated there.  Arrived at Bear Hollow in September 2008.
