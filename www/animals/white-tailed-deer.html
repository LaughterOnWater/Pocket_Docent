White-Tailed Deer in Athens

Deer are so numerous around Athens, in our suburbs and farms, that it helps for us to be vigilant while driving, especially at dawn and dusk when lighting can be tricky. You can learn more than the casual observer by clicking the buttons below...

Taxonomy

Order Artiodactyla
Family Cervidae
Genus Odocoileus
Species virginianus

Diet

  • In the wild, deer eat a wide variety of vegetable matter.  Leaves, shoots, grasses and legumes all make up the diet.  Acorns are a major diet component.
  • At Bear Hollow, the deer eat a mix of horse sweet feed, lettuce, carrots and other vegetables.  Strawberries and grapes are a favorite treat.

Range and Habitat

  • Mostly wooded areas, but predominantly the edge habitat (where two habitats meet).  Also found in urban environments, river corridors, swamps and mountains.
  • In North America, found in the entire eastern half of the continent.
  • In Georgia, they range statewide and are found in every county.

Physical Features

  • Coat reddish brown during the spring and summer, but turns brownish gray in the late fall and winter.
  • Underside of tail is white.
  • Body length is about seven feet from nose to rump, and deer stand a little over three feet tall at the shoulder.
  • Bucks weigh in at between 120 and 230 pounds, and does weigh between 90 and 200 pounds.

Life Span

  • In the wild, deer may live to be almost ten years old, the average being five or six years.
  • In captivity, deer can live much longer – regularly to 14, but occasionally as many as 20 years.

Reproduction

  • Females enter ‘rut’ in the late fall and go through the winter breeding with the dominant male in the area.
  • Mature bucks will spar with each other to determine their place in the hierarchy.  They ram each other and enter pushing matches with their antlers.
  • One to three fawns are born to a female several months later, in May or June.

Social Behavior

  • Deer have numerous scent glands.  They use these to communicate with other deer in places called rubs (on trees or rocks) and scrapes (in cleared places on the ground). 
  • Considered to be a social animal that usually travels in small groups.  Generally separated out by the sexes.  Does travel in small units, often with fawns, but bucks either travel alone or in small bachelor group.
  • Deer communicate with various audible noises including grunts, bleats and snorting.   Additionally, when a white-tail deer is spooked it will raise its tail to warn the other deer in the area that can see it.

Conservation

  • White tails are a species of little conservation concern.  They are usually so numerous that most states have hunting regulations in place for their harvest.
  • A century ago the deer population was severely depressed for various reasons.  Conservation efforts have since been so successful that, combined with the lack of natural predators for deer, there are now 30 million deer estimated to live in the US.  Hunting and population control methods are necessary to control this species which threatens farmers’ crops (being excellent fence jumpers), native fauna (given that they can decimate plant populations and even destroy attempts at re-foresting which then drives out other native fauna, even bears, by depriving them of their food source), as well as human and deer life due to the high number of vehicular crashes caused by deer.  These concerns actually make deer one of the most dangerous animals in North America because of the potential damage they can cause by their sheer numbers.

 Interpretive Information

  • Fawns are born with light colored spots all over their body, and no scent.  This helps them to hide in the leaf litter while their mother is out looking for food.
  • Bucks re-grow their antlers every year.
  • Deer are ruminants with a four chambered stomach.  This allows them to eat a wide variety of food in a hurry then digest it later.
  • They are prey to many animals in the zoo, including (potentially) the American alligator, the Black Bear, and the Bobcats.  Bald Eagles and Golden Eagles can even prey on fawns.  However, their most effective natural predators are wolves, cougars and American alligators.
