Taxonomy

Order Galliformes
Family Meleagrididae
Genus Meleagris
Species gallopavo

Diet

  • In the wild, turkeys eat nearly anything that is small enough to fit in their mouths: small animals, seeds, nuts, grains insects.
  • At Bear Hollow, the turkeys eat various seeds and grains, as well as scratch food for domestic birds. They also eat any small animals, insects and acorns they find in their enclosure.

Range and Habitat

  • Wild turkeys are found throughout the entire eastern half of the United States and have a patchy distribution throughout the western half of the US.
  • In Georgia, wild turkeys can be found statewide, except in the immediate metro Atlanta area.
  • Turkeys require an area that has a mix of open and covered space. Hardwood forests are a favored habitat, but they can also be found in meadows, swamps and

Physical Features

  • Dark brown to blackish feathers cover the body.  Feathers often have a bronze or greenish shimmer to them.  Head of both sexes is featherless.
  • Long reddish legs.  Males develop large pointed spurs on the backs of their ankles as they age.  Wing feathers are distinctively barred with black and white.
  • Males typically weigh between 11 and 24 lbs.  Females are slightly smaller, weighing between 6.5 and 12 lbs.  Wingspans are between four and five feet.

Life Span

  • In the wild, turkeys generally life three to four years.
  • In captivity, they can live as long as ten to twelve years.

Reproduction

  • Courtship, and the males’ extravagant dancing and calling, begins in March and continues through Spring.
  • Females lay ten to fourteen eggs in a shallow, leaf-lined nest hidden among brush or grass.  The poults hatch after about 28 days.

Social Behavior

  • Turkeys are polygamous, with a male having a group of hens and juveniles with him.
  • During the day, turkeys are foraging on the ground looking for food.  At night, the turkeys fly up into the trees and roost.  This keeps them safe from predation, and they will fly back down to the ground after the sun has come up.

Conservation

  • In the early 1900’s, the entire turkey population was less than 30,000.  Today, the total population is estimated to be over 7 million birds.
  • They are a species of little conservation concern, and 49 states have turkey hunting seasons.

 Interpretive Information

  • Adult male turkeys are called ‘toms’, or ‘gobblers’.  Females are ‘hens’.  Immature males are called ‘jakes’, and juvenile turkeys are known as ‘poults’.
  • The colors on their heads change with the turkey’s excitement level.
  • The various knobs and bumps on a turkey’s head all have names.  The dangling piece over the beak is the snood.  The bumps along the head and face are carnuncles.  The fleshy lobes at the base of the neck are the wattles.
  • The Wild Turkey was Benjamin Franklin’s choice for our National Bird.
  • When Europeans arrived, they made it one of only two domestic birds native to the Americas—the Muscovy duck shares the distinction.
  • Wild Turkeys had been hunted nearly to extinction by the 1900’s. Wild turkey reintroduction programs began in the 1940s, and the birds were relocated to areas where populations had been decimated but woodlands were recovering. Such efforts worked so well that wild turkeys now live in areas where they may not have occurred when Europeans first reached the Americas.
  • Domestic turkeys have white-tipped tails because they are the descendants of a Mexican subspecies that was taken to Europe for domestication in the early 16th century. The feature distinguishes them from most modern wild turkeys.
