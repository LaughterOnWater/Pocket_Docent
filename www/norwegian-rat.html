Norwegian Fancy Rat / Domestic Rat

Taxonomy:

Order

Rodentia

Family

Muridae

Genus

Rattus

Species

norvegicus

Diet:

  • In urban environments they eat mainly discarded human food
  • Near the sea they have been observed catching fish
  • They will also eat chicks, mice, birds, & small lizards
  • At Bear Hollow, the Rats eat commercially prepared rodent diet, various nuts and seeds, and fresh fruits and vegetables.

Range and Habitat:

  • Can be found on every continent of the world except Antarctica
  • Before they spread across the globe they were found only in Asia where they live primarily in forests and brushy areas
  • Today they can be found living in a variety of habitats, such as dumps, sewers, open fields & woodlands, & basements. Basically where ever there are people
  • Their average home range or territories is 164 feet

Physical Features:

  • Covered with coarse, brownish fur (sometimes splotched with black or white hairs) on their dorsal surface, which usually lightens to a gray or tan color nearing the underside. Various strains of these rats bred in captivity may be white, brown, or black.
  • The ears and tail are bald
  • Avg. length is 16 inches. Avg. weight is almost a pound.

Life Span:

  • Up to 2 years in the wild, and up to 4 years in captivity
  • Rattus norvegicus is preyed on by any number of carnivorous mammals, birds, and reptiles. Humans also kill very large numbers of Norway rats as pests.

Reproduction:

  • The mating system of R. norvegicus is best described as Polygynandrous. Social animals, Norway rats tend to breed in large groups.
  • Although not technically a seasonal breeder, a mating increase occurs in the warmer months of the year. An average female is capable of giving birth approximately 8 pups, about seven times per year. Each litter has a short gestation period of 22 to 24 days. The young are very small and underdeveloped. It takes 14 to 17 days for the young's eyes to open. They are weaned at 3 to 4 weeks, and the young then leave the nest. 
  • Males usually reach sexual maturity at 3 months and females at 4.

Social Behavior:

  • Often, the litters of numerous females will occupy the same nest, and all the young are cared for by the adults, regardless of who the true mothers are. This communal care makes the species something of a cooperative breeder.
  • Live in large nests or burrows with many others
  • They are vocal, and use visual cues such as body posture when communicating
  • The hierarchy of these groups is based on size

Conservation:

  • These animals are not a conservation concern. In fact, humans spend a great deal of effort trying to eradicate them.

Interpretive Information:

  • Norway rats are excellent competitors and will readily drive out competing rat species, such as Rattus rattus. Because of their foraging habits, Norway rats act as seed dispersers. Their burrows also tend to aerate the soil. As prey, they help to sustain predator populations. Norway rats are communal species with humans.
  • They are able to sense very minute vibrations in the ground, and feel their way through total darkness with their paws and whiskers.
  • First introduced into the United States around 1775, the Norway rat has now spread throughout the contiguous 48 states.
  • Examination of a wild R. norvegicus stomach in Germany revealed 4000 items, most of which were plants, although studies have shown that Norway rats prefer meat when given the option.
  • Rats are very clean animals, they spend several hours per day grooming themselves.
  • They can chew through metals such as copper and aluminum.
  • They have an excellent memory. Once they learn a path they will never forget it.
